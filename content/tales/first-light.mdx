Stories don't begin with the first sentence. They begin with the moment something notices you.

Most people think dawn is a promise — a clean start, a warm breath, a gentle hand on the shoulder. But light in Verasanth is older than morning. It doesn't rise. It reveals.

A lantern flame remembers every face that ever leaned close to it. A hearth remembers every voice that ever whispered secrets beside it. Even the smallest spark carries a history of who fed it, who feared it, and who tried to hide from what it showed.

Light here is not comfort. It is attention.

Walk the streets before sunrise and you'll see it: the way shadows stretch toward you instead of away, the way windows glow a heartbeat too early, the way the city seems to inhale before the day begins.

That isn't magic. It's memory.

The first light of the day is not the sun. It's the city opening its eyes.

And if you feel watched in that moment, don't be alarmed. It isn't judging you.

It's deciding whether your story has already begun.
