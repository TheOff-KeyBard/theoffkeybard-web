Halden Marr is rarely the first to arrive.

But he is almost always the last to leave.

Where others see a lost cause, he remains — not out of stubbornness, but because departure, to him, feels like a second wound.

He does not command.

He does not persuade.

He stays.

There are those in Verasanth who cannot be saved. This is understood. Accepted. Even expected.

Halden does not argue this.

He simply refuses to let them face it alone.

His hands carry warmth where there should be none. Not enough to undo what has been done — never that — but enough to hold the moment in place a little longer. Enough for breath to steady. Enough for pain to loosen its grip.

Some call this mercy.

Others call it denial.

Halden calls it necessary.

The city feeds on what is abandoned.

He has made it his purpose to leave as little behind as possible.

It is not a battle he expects to win.

But it is one he refuses to stop fighting.
